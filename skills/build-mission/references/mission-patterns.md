# Mission Patterns

Use this reference when drafting, comparing, or improving mission statements.

## Mission Versus Nearby Statements

- Mission: why the work exists now and what change it creates for whom.
- Vision: the future state the work wants to make possible.
- Tagline: a memorable external phrase, often shorter and less complete.
- Values: behavioral principles for how the team works.
- Strategy: choices about where to play, how to win, and what to prioritize.

A mission can inspire, but it must also constrain. If it cannot help decide what not to build, it is probably too vague.

## Core Formula

Most useful mission statements combine four elements:

```text
Help <audience> solve/change <problem or state> by/through <mechanism>, so that <intended outcome>.
```

Not every final sentence needs all four parts, but the thinking should cover them.

## Strong Mission Traits

- Names a primary audience or beneficiary.
- Points at a concrete problem, unmet need, or intended change.
- Implies the product or operating mechanism.
- Is narrow enough to reject off-mission work.
- Can be linked to success signals or feedback loops.
- Uses plain language that the audience would recognize.

## Weak Mission Signals

- “Empower everyone” without naming who or how.
- “Transform the future” without a concrete problem.
- A list of features instead of a reason for existing.
- A market category statement with no user pain.
- Multiple competing missions across README, pitch, and roadmap.
- A mission that only describes internal ambition.

## Three Useful Draft Shapes

### Clear / Direct

Best when the project needs operational clarity.

```text
Help <specific audience> <do important job> without <specific pain>.
```

Example:

```text
Help rural clinic nurses turn handwritten visit notes into accurate patient summaries before the end of each shift.
```

### Strategic / Product-Led

Best when the mission should clarify product positioning.

```text
Give <audience> a <mechanism/product category> for <job>, so they can <outcome>.
```

Example:

```text
Give independent clinics a review-first documentation workflow so care teams can finish accurate visit summaries the same day.
```

### Aspirational But Concrete

Best when the mission needs more public-facing energy without losing specificity.

```text
Make <important outcome> accessible to <audience> by removing <specific barrier>.
```

Example:

```text
Make same-day clinical documentation realistic for rural care teams by removing the manual rewrite burden from every shift.
```

## Stress-Test Questions

Use these before recommending a mission:

1. Would the primary audience recognize themselves in it?
2. Does it point to a real problem or meaningful change?
3. Does it imply what the project actually does?
4. Could it reject a tempting but off-mission feature?
5. Could the team measure progress toward it?
6. Would it still make sense six months from now if the roadmap changes?
7. Is it too broad for the current project stage?

## Choosing Among Options

Choose the mission that has the best trade-off power, not the prettiest wording.

- Pick clear/direct when the team lacks focus.
- Pick strategic/product-led when positioning and roadmap alignment matter most.
- Pick aspirational but concrete when external narrative matters, but only if the audience and problem remain explicit.

## Supporting Copy

After choosing the mission, supporting copy can expand the logic:

```text
We serve <audience> because <problem evidence>. Today, <current pain>. Our work focuses on <mechanism/scope>. We know we are succeeding when <success signal>.
```

Keep this separate from the one-sentence mission so the mission remains memorable.
